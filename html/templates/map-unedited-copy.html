Our Goals

The Open Data Impact Map is intended to:

  • Demonstrate the value of open government data in a range of applications;
  • Identify key trends and best practices in open data use; and
  • Provide a basis for further analysis of the impact of open data globally.

The Map makes it possible to explore open data use trends through a number of filters and visualizations, and is structured to facilitate comparative analysis by region, country, or city.

Methodology

Eligibility

The Open Data Impact Map includes organizations that:

  • are companies, non-profits, or developer groups; and
  • use open government data to develop products and services, improve operations, inform strategy and/or conduct research.

We define open government data as publicly available data that is produced or commissioned by governments and can be accessed and reused by anyone, free of charge.

Data Collection

We are collecting open data use cases in three ways:

  • Research: The Center’s research team is identifying and curating examples from publicly available sources, interviews, and publications. This includes the Sunlight Foundation’s crowdsourced list of projects using open data for social impact, the GovLab’s OD500 studies, lists compiled by World Bank, and more.
  • Regional Supporters: Our Regional Supporters around the world send us case studies of open data use based on their work and local expertise.
  • Online & Mobile Survey: The online and mobile survey allows organizations using open data to provide information directly. Our survey has been informed by the GovLab at NYU’s Open Data 500 study.

All information collected is vetted, curated, and transformed into interoperable data for display on the Map. The data can be downloaded from the website in machine readable formats.

Limitations

The Open Data Impact Map is designed to be a resource for further analysis of the impact of open data globally. It is not in itself an attempt to rate, assess, or quantify the economic or social value of open data. The use cases collected for the map are not a random or necessarily representative sample of all uses of open data globally.
